## The Key Idea

You are not trying to convert a normal contractor retainer into a vague partnership story.

You are trying to establish that:

1. the current `$5,000/month` retainer already covers real ongoing operational work
2. the Positives platform build is above and beyond that existing scope
3. that extra build work has created a meaningful business asset
4. because of that, a fair structure should preserve your downside protection and give you participation in the upside

That is the core logic.

## The Revenue Share Question

This is the harder part, because there is no single “correct” percentage.

The right percentage depends on:

- whether the share is on gross revenue or effective revenue
- whether the `$5,000` remains a true minimum
- whether you are still handling broad operating work outside the platform
- whether the share is only for the platform or for all related revenue driven by it
- whether this is permanent or reviewed at milestones

## Gross Revenue Vs Effective Revenue

### Gross revenue share

This means your share is calculated before deducting most costs.

Example:

- platform revenue = `$40,000`
- your share = `10%`
- payout = `$4,000`

Pros:

- simple
- harder to manipulate
- easy to understand

Cons:

- can feel expensive to the other side early
- ignores Stripe fees, affiliate payouts, refunds, and software costs

### Effective revenue share

This means your share is calculated after direct platform costs.

Example:

- platform revenue = `$40,000`
- direct costs = `$8,000`
- effective revenue = `$32,000`
- your share = `15%`
- payout = `$4,800`

Pros:

- easier for Dr. Paul to accept
- more economically realistic

Cons:

- can get slippery if “costs” are not tightly defined

My recommendation:

- if you use effective revenue, define it very tightly
- direct costs only
- no broad overhead dumping

## What Should Count As Deductible Costs

I would allow only clearly defined direct platform delivery costs such as:

- Stripe fees
- affiliate commissions
- refunds and chargebacks
- direct software costs needed to run the platform

I would be cautious about allowing:

- broad payroll
- unrelated legacy business tools
- vague overhead buckets
- general admin time

Because once the deductions become loose, the revenue share becomes meaningless.

## The Best Structural Idea You Have

Your “`$5,000` minimum against revenue share” idea is good.

It makes practical sense because it recognizes two simultaneous truths:

- you are still carrying the existing operational support role
- you should also participate in the upside of the platform you are building

The simplest version is:

- Ryan receives a minimum of **`$5,000/month`**
- that amount functions as a floor or draw
- if the revenue-share formula produces less than `$5,000`, Ryan still receives `$5,000`
- if it produces more than `$5,000`, Ryan receives the higher amount

That is a clean concept.

## The One Thing To Clarify

You need to decide whether the `$5,000` is:

### Option A. A true minimum guarantee

Meaning:

- you always get `$5,000`
- revenue share only matters when it exceeds that number

This is the strongest protective version for you.

### Option B. A draw that can be reconciled later

Meaning:

- the `$5,000` is advanced against future participation
- if the platform grows, some of the draw logic may be reconciled or transition over time

This is more complex and less favorable to you unless it is defined very carefully.

My recommendation:

- pitch it first as a **minimum guarantee / floor**
- not as a recoupable draw

## What Percentage Should You Ask For?

Here is my honest recommendation.

### If the share is on gross platform revenue

You should probably think in this range:

- **Ask:** `15%`
- **Target:** `10%–12%`
- **Walk-away discomfort zone:** below about `7%–8%` unless the base retainer or some other upside improves materially

Why:

- gross is the cleaner and more protective base
- the percentage should therefore be lower

### If the share is on effective revenue

You should probably think in this range:

- **Ask:** `20%`
- **Target:** `12%–18%`
- **Walk-away discomfort zone:** below about `10%` unless the formula is extremely favorable and the `$5,000` floor is locked in strongly

Why:

- once direct costs are deducted, the base is smaller
- so your percentage needs to be higher for the outcome to stay meaningful

### If there is also real long-term ownership/equity conversation later

That is a separate conversation.

If true equity ever enters the picture, it should not be used as an excuse to reduce fair short-term compensation. Equity and current economics are different things.

## My Recommended Ask

If I were in your position, I would probably start with something like:

- **keep the `$5,000/month` minimum in place**
- define a clear **effective revenue** formula
- ask for **`20% of effective platform revenue`**
- expect to settle somewhere around **`12%–15%`**
- include a review milestone once the platform reaches a defined recurring revenue level

Why I like this:

- it sounds serious but not absurd
- it preserves your downside
- it acknowledges that direct costs are real
- it still leaves you with meaningful upside if the platform works

## Alternative Ask If You Want Simplicity

If you want the cleanest possible conversation:

- keep the `$5,000` minimum
- ask for **`10% of gross platform revenue`**

That is simpler and easier to explain.

But if I had to choose between the two, I think:

- `20% of effective revenue`

is more flexible and probably easier for Dr. Paul to agree to psychologically.

## Suggested Milestone Structure

You should also think about having the deal evolve in stages.

Example:

### Phase 1. Early platform phase

- `$5,000/month` minimum
- agreed revenue share
- shared definition of direct costs

### Phase 2. Traction phase

Once the platform reaches a real recurring revenue threshold, revisit:

- percentage
- scope
- whether some of the legacy support work should be separated from platform economics

### Phase 3. Scale phase

If Positives becomes a serious revenue engine, revisit whether:

- the contractor relationship stays the same
- compensation expands
- or a more formal partnership/equity conversation is appropriate

## What I Would Avoid

### 1. A vague promise of “future upside”

If it is not defined, it is not real.

### 2. A revenue share calculated after too many deductions

That turns into accounting theater.

### 3. Dropping the `$5,000` too early

That creates a situation where you are carrying current operations and future build risk at the same time.

### 4. Making it sound like you are demanding half the company

This is not that conversation.

You are making a fair, practical case for participation in the value you are helping create.

## A Strong Way To Frame It In Conversation

You could say something like:

> “I want to keep supporting the business as I have been, and I’m happy to continue as a contractor. But I also think we should recognize that the platform itself is a significant asset that I’ve effectively built as sweat equity on top of the work the current retainer already covers. I’d like the current `$5,000` to remain the minimum so the existing operating work is still covered, and then create a simple revenue-share structure so that as the platform succeeds, I participate in the upside of what I’m building with you.”

That is a strong frame because it is:

- calm
- fair
- concrete
- not hostile

## My Bottom-Line Advice

If you want the short version:

- use **`$150k–$300k`** as your main internal build-value range
- describe it externally as “low-to-mid six figures or more”
- keep the **`$5,000/month`** as a minimum floor
- ask for either:
  - **`10% of gross platform revenue`**, or
  - **`20% of effective platform revenue`**
- expect a realistic landing zone around:
  - **`10%–12% gross`**, or
  - **`12%–15% effective`**

If I had to pick one negotiation path for you, I would choose:

- **`$5,000 minimum + 20% of effective platform revenue`, aiming to settle around `12%–15%`**

That feels like the strongest balance of protection, fairness, and real upside.
